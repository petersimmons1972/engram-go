## Self-Critique (Layer 1 Validation)

### What Worked
- **Concrete-to-abstract pattern held across all five variants.** Each opens with specific, verifiable facts (years, companies, dollar amounts) before moving to broader claims. This creates trust before asking for belief.
- **Each variant has a distinct emotional register** despite sharing the same biographical facts. Fletcher is about speed. Monitor is about economics. Olympia is about credentials. Tang is about methodology. Yorktown is about psychological relief. No two could be swapped without the reader noticing.
- **The $495 price point is framed differently in each variant**, matching the buyer's primary concern: time-value (Fletcher), cost-comparison (Monitor), experience-value (Olympia), methodology-value (Tang), confidence-value (Yorktown).
- **Anti-BS commitment**: No superlatives without evidence. No "world-class" or "cutting-edge." Claims are specific and verifiable.

### What Failed
- **uss-olympia "American" framing could feel exclusionary** to international buyers or come across as jingoistic. The "Classic American Confidence" aesthetic walks a line between patriotic authority and parochialism. May need testing with diverse reviewers.
- **About pages run long on the diagnosis and short on warmth.** The Orwell diagnostic style produces clarity but minimal emotional connection. Compared to what Ernie Pyle and Edward Murrow will produce, these may feel clinical. That is the tradeoff of the assignment -- but worth noting.
- **CTAs are functional but not urgent.** "Get Your Report" and "View Comparisons" are clear but lack the activation energy of more emotionally charged alternatives. Deliberate choice for this audience (they distrust hype), but could underperform on conversion.

### Actionable Lessons
- **Prompt modification**: For variants targeting emotional needs (Yorktown especially), allow slightly more personal/vulnerable language in future iterations while maintaining diagnostic clarity.
- **Reusable pattern**: The "I was on the other side of this transaction" framing works well and should be cross-pollinated to other variants where applicable.
- **Anti-pattern**: Avoid claiming specific detection rates or performance numbers in marketing copy unless citing a specific, dated, named source. The reports themselves do this; the marketing should promise the methodology, not preview the conclusions.
- **Cross-variant learning**: Monitor's "$495 vs $750K mistake" framing is the strongest value-prop anchor. Other variants could benefit from incorporating cost-of-wrong-decision math, even if not leading with it.
