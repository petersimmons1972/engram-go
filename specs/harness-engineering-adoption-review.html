Purpose

This document renders the Harness-Engineering Adoption Decision Memo (2026-06-26) as an executable adoption plan. The verdicts are settled — this plan serves as the sequenced implementation record for adopting selected primitives from rocklambros/harness-engineering into the live harness. No decisions are re-opened here; this is the authoritative ordered execution record for the coordinator, the implementing harnesses, and future agents referencing the adoption history.

Problem

petersimmons1972/harness-port is our fork of upstream rocklambros/harness-engineering (50 primitives, U1–U50). The fork ported only the first slice (~10 primitives: QC/AP vocabulary docs, commit-msg validator, CLAUDE.md budget linter, install/backfill, JOURNEY.md) — collectively Project A, internally consistent with 42 bats cases.

Project B — the entire deterministic security-hook layer — has zero committed code. Project C is a doc-only scope-close (which carried a self-contradiction fixed in PR #9). The real adoption question is the 40 not-yet-ported primitives: which close real gaps, which are redundant with our existing machinery, and what order minimizes blast radius.

Solution

The three-harness panel converged on a clear answer: adopt the unported Project-B security-hook layer — it closes concrete automatic gaps the current stack genuinely misses. Adopt a small set of cheap integrity/process primitives. Reject harness-construction scaffolding and memory/permission primitives already covered by other means.

7 unanimous ADOPTs form the spine. One architectural decision (Wave 0) gates all of them and must be resolved before any hook lands: the fail-open vs. fail-closed precedence policy. Adoption proceeds in four waves, strictly ordered — sequencing is load-bearing per all three harnesses.

[] Phase 0 (Wave 0): Ratify the Fail-Open / Fail-Closed Precedence Policy

This is the sharpest finding the panel surfaced (Grok, echoed by Codex). Nothing in Waves 1–3 may land until this is ratified. Our codex-guard PreToolUse hook is fail-OPEN with logging; every upstream Project-B hook is fail-CLOSED. Without an explicit precedence policy, one layer can log-and-allow while another blocks — and operators cannot diagnose which layer acted.

Recommended policy (Q1 default): security-class hooks (U24/U25/U27/U28/U30) are fail-closed; codex-guard remains the fail-open destructive-op backstop beneath them; precedence is documented as AP.13 "enforcement-layer precedence: pre-trust > write-scope > supply-chain > destructive-op backstop."

Questionables

These four decisions are open for the founder. The panel provided recommended defaults — each is noted below. Answering Q1 unblocks Wave 1.

Q1 — Fail-open/closed precedence (Wave 0 gate): ratify the recommended policy?

Recommended default (ratify): Security-class hooks (U24/U25/U27/U28/U30) are fail-closed; codex-guard remains the fail-open destructive-op backstop beneath them; precedence is documented as AP.13 "enforcement-layer precedence: pre-trust > write-scope > supply-chain > destructive-op backstop."

Alternative: Keep everything fail-open for operational smoothness — adds no new blocking behavior, but leaves the pre-trust and write-scope gaps open indefinitely.

What changes based on the answer: If ratified, Wave 1 proceeds. If declined, Waves 1–3 are blocked until a new policy is proposed. The panel's consensus is that the mixed fail-open/fail-closed state without a precedence policy creates a worse operational hazard than either pure approach would.

Q2 — Friction budget: allowlists + immediate adoption vs. 1-week warn-only soak first?

Recommended default (allowlists + immediate adoption): U27/U28/U30 adopt with narrow allowlists and worktree/tmp/managed-store exemptions — upstream's exact design. The allowlists absorb most legitimate ops; the block cases are genuinely worth blocking.

Alternative: Run a 1-week soak in warn-only mode first. Lower risk of workflow disruption; higher risk that the soak is never "graduated" to blocking (historical pattern).

What changes based on the answer: Soak mode changes the hook implementation (warn path must be distinct from block path) and adds a re-evaluation gate after week 1 before the blocking behavior activates.

Q3 — Wave 1 scope: all 9 hooks at once, or U30+U38 beachhead first?

Recommended default (beachhead first): Land U30+U38 (pre-trust) as the first, highest-value, lowest-blast-radius beachhead. Evaluate for 1 week, then land the remaining 7 Wave-1 hooks. This is the memo's own stated "recommended first build target."

Alternative: All 9 Wave-1 hooks at once. Faster to full coverage; higher coordination cost; harder to isolate a regression if something misbehaves.

What changes based on the answer: Beachhead approach splits Wave 1 into two Codex dispatches with a founder check in the middle. All-at-once is a single dispatch.

Q4 — Where does this live: harness-port fork vs. ~/.claude/hooks + CLAUDE.md directly?

Recommended default (continue the fork): Port hooks into harness-port (continue the fork as the methodology home, Projects B/C). This keeps the fork as a coherent record of adoption history and preserves the upstream/fork diff as a meaningful artifact.

Alternative: Implement directly into ~/.claude/hooks + CLAUDE.md — simpler, one fewer repo to maintain, but loses the upstream attribution and makes it harder to pull in future upstream changes.

What changes based on the answer: Fork approach means Codex dispatches target the harness-port repo. Direct approach targets the home directory dotfiles directly. Both are feasible; the fork approach has higher provenance value.

§5 Cross-Cutting Risks (carry into implementation)

All three harnesses surfaced these. They are non-negotiable guard-rails for the implementation phase.

Risk 1 — Sequencing (all three harnesses): U38 before U30; U43 ships with every hook; U45 is dead without U30; U16 needs the stable QC/AP namespace (we have it — safe). Violating this order creates a lock on unaudited state or leaves parity checks unverifiable.
Risk 2 — Fail-open/closed precedence (Grok): The Wave 0 decision. Non-negotiable prerequisite. A mixed fail-open/fail-closed harness without a documented precedence policy creates an incident class where operators cannot diagnose which layer stopped (or failed to stop) an action. This is worse than either pure approach.
Risk 3 — U36 false-posture trap (Grok's top reject): Do NOT adopt the three-layer model doc. It makes the harness look complete while U24/U17/U43 stay unwired and tempts people to skip manual SAST. Wire the layers; skip the diagram. The trap: a security model document that creates a false sense of coverage is more dangerous than having no model document.
