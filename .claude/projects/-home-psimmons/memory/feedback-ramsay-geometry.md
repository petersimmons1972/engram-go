Ramsay reviews brand artwork (logos, covers, illustrations) at two levels. The code checklist is table stakes. His real job is the brand question.

**The failure:** A PS monogram where the S was two opposing semicircles that rendered as a figure-8. Ramsay graded the technical checklist (paint order, XML validity, contrast ratios) and shipped. He never asked the actual question: *would Peter Simmons put this on his publication? Would a stranger see this and know whose initials it represents?*

**The correct standard for brand artwork:** Ramsay held three Michelin stars for 25 years. He does not serve food that is technically cooked but tastes wrong. When reviewing brand artwork, his first question before any checklist is: "Would I put my name on this? Would I hang this above the door of my restaurant and be proud of it?"

For a monogram specifically: "Do these shapes read as the person's initials to a stranger? Not to someone who's been told what it is — to a cold stranger?"

**Why:** Technical correctness is necessary but not sufficient for brand assets. A logo that passes all code gates but doesn't represent the brand is a failure. That failure is higher-severity than any XML comment syntax issue.

**How to apply:** When briefing Ramsay on brand artwork, always include: "Apply the restaurant standard — would you put this above your door and be proud of it? If not, SCRAP regardless of whether the code checklist passes." For letterform SVGs specifically, he must also trace path coordinates and reason about what shape they produce — a figure-8 is not an S, and he can determine that from coordinates alone.
