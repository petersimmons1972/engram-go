# Persuasion & Influence Models

Ethical frameworks for influencing customer decisions. These models explain how people respond to appeals, authority, and social dynamics.

## Cialdini's Principles of Influence

### Reciprocity Principle

People feel obligated to return favors. Give first, and people want to give back. Generosity triggers reciprocal obligation.

**Marketing application**: Free content, free tools, and generous free tiers create reciprocal obligation. Give value before asking for anything. Lead with generosity.

---

### Commitment & Consistency

Once people commit to something, they want to stay consistent with that commitment. Small choices compound into bigger ones.

**Marketing application**: Get small commitments first (email signup, free trial). People who've taken one step are more likely to take the next.

---

### Authority Bias

People defer to experts and authority figures. Credentials and expertise create trust and influence.

**Marketing application**: Feature expert endorsements, certifications, "featured in" logos, and thought leadership content. Establish authority early.

---

### Liking / Similarity Bias

People say yes to those they like and those similar to themselves. Similarity breeds affinity.

**Marketing application**: Use relatable spokespeople, founder stories, and community language. "Built by marketers for marketers" signals similarity and in-group belonging.

---

### Social Proof / Bandwagon Effect

Consensus creates confidence. People follow what others are doing and assume popularity signals quality.

**Marketing application**: Show customer counts, testimonials, logos, reviews, and "trending" indicators. Real numbers create credibility.

---

### Scarcity / Urgency Heuristic

Limited availability increases perceived value. Scarcity signals desirability and creates urgency.

**Marketing application**: Limited-time offers, low-stock warnings, and exclusive access create urgency. Only use when genuine. Fake scarcity damages trust.

---

## Request Techniques

### Foot-in-the-Door Technique

Start with a small request, then escalate. Compliance with small requests leads to compliance with larger ones.

**Marketing application**: Free trial → paid plan → annual plan → enterprise. Each step builds on the last, increasing commitment.

---

### Door-in-the-Face Technique

Start with an unreasonably large request, then retreat to what you actually want. The contrast makes the second request seem reasonable.

**Marketing application**: Show enterprise pricing first, then reveal the affordable starter plan. The contrast makes it feel like a deal.

---

## Decision Framing

### Loss Aversion / Prospect Theory

Losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good. Avoiding loss is more motivating than achieving gain.

**Marketing application**: Frame in terms of what they'll lose by not acting. "Don't miss out on 40% faster workflows" beats "Gain 40% faster workflows."

---

### Anchoring Effect

The first number people see heavily influences subsequent judgments. Initial anchors create reference points.

**Marketing application**: Show the higher price first (original price, competitor price, enterprise tier) to anchor expectations downward.

---

### Decoy Effect

Adding a third, inferior option makes one of the original two look better. The decoy shifts preference.

**Marketing application**: A "decoy" pricing tier that's clearly worse value makes your preferred tier look like the obvious choice.

---

### Framing Effect

How something is presented changes how it's perceived. Same facts, different frames create different reactions.

**Marketing application**: "90% success rate" vs. "10% failure rate" are identical but feel different. Frame positively. "Fast checkout" vs. "Slow competitors."

---

### Contrast Effect

Things seem different depending on what they're compared to. Context shifts perception.

**Marketing application**: Show the "before" state clearly. The contrast with your "after" makes improvements vivid and valuable.

---

## Identity & Belonging

### Unity Principle

Shared identity drives influence. "One of us" is powerful. In-group identity creates loyalty.

**Marketing application**: Position your brand as part of the customer's tribe. Use insider language and shared values. Create community belonging.

---

## Strategic Sequencing

### Hick's Law

Decision time increases with the number and complexity of choices. More options = slower decisions = more abandonment.

**Marketing application**: Simplify choices. One clear CTA beats three. Fewer form fields beat more. Reduce cognitive load at decision points.

---

### AIDA Funnel

Attention → Interest → Desire → Action. The classic customer journey model.

**Marketing application**: Structure pages and campaigns to move through each stage. Capture attention before building desire. Build logic into progression.

---

### Nudge Theory / Choice Architecture

Small changes in how choices are presented significantly influence decisions without restricting choice.

**Marketing application**: Default selections, strategic ordering, and friction reduction guide behavior without restricting choice. Design choice architecture thoughtfully.

---

## Motivation & Behavior

### BJ Fogg Behavior Model

Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt. All three must be present for action.

**Marketing application**: High motivation but hard to do = won't happen. Easy to do but no prompt = won't happen. Design for all three simultaneously.

---

### EAST Framework

Make desired behaviors: Easy, Attractive, Social, Timely.

**Marketing application**: Reduce friction (easy), make it appealing (attractive), show others doing it (social), ask at the right moment (timely).

---

### Activation Energy

The initial energy required to start something. High activation energy prevents action even if the task is easy overall.

**Marketing application**: Reduce starting friction. Pre-fill forms, offer templates, show quick wins. Make the first step trivially easy.

---

## Warning: Ethical Boundaries

These influence models are powerful. Using them ethically means:

- **Transparency**: Be honest about offers, pricing, and limitations
- **Customer benefit first**: Influence should serve their interests
- **Long-term thinking**: Short-term tricks damage trust forever
- **Consent**: Respect opt-out and preference choices
- **Authenticity**: Don't fake testimonials, social proof, or scarcity

The strongest persuasion aligns your interests with customers' interests. That's sustainable influence.
